   "The Valley" was written using derivative material from my previous work, a Brass Quintet. The goal was to continue writing in a contrapuntal and horizontal style and again without any regard for key signature, however, despite the lack of key and this technique's association with atonal works, my goal here was to compose a satisfylingly sing-song-ey piece––perhaps in defiance of the academic penchant towards atonality, dissonance, and serialism. The bounding arpeggiations draw much inspiration from Justin Hurwitz's work on the film "La La Land," and the opening solo and trio sprouted from my childhood recollections of watching Hayao Miyazaki's film "Kiki's Delivery Service" of which the scenes overlooking the town by the shore as well as the scene featuring a laggardly dog and Kiki's pet cat Jiji played a prominent role.

My Wind Quintet, "The Valley," was originally written for brass and as a prelude to my Brass Quintet. The following was the initial sketch.

   Written for my graduation recital at the University of Lethbridge and under the influence of silicon valley grindset culture. The piece opens with indulgent optimism—the bravado of a startup founder hard on the heels of securing their first angel list seed, only to then be mauled and torn down by a bearish dip in morale. Nevertheless, "real artists ship," and therefore sprint to realize their exit strategy. No matter the scale of cognitive dissonance, a founder inevitably (or hopefully) surges past the reality distortion field, persisting through periods of self-collapse and black swan catastrophe, they ride the bull to a triumphant victory. This is my first multi-movement work.

"Source" marks what I would consider my first successful attempt at applying formal structure to a composition, although not without difficulty and headache. The work was conceived in a fugato style, alternating between soft contrapuntal sections and grande chordal sections. My intention was to follow a traditional AABA structure using the B section as a tonal and timbral shift, however, I ran into the problem of excitedly adding whatever new material I could muster to my "contrasting B section." It was much to my dismay when I came to realized my B section was a different piece entirely! I have since split the sections into two pieces. "Source" is the reduction of nearly 9 minutes of material.
